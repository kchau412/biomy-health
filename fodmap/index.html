The food guide

What can I actually eat?

FODMAP, in plain language. No science degree required. Here's a simple traffic-light guide to what's usually fine, what depends, and what tends to cause trouble.

FODMAP is a framework developed by Monash University that identifies the short-chain carbohydrates most likely to trigger gut symptoms.

What even is a FODMAP?

You got told to try low-FODMAP and immediately googled "what is a saccharide." Same. Here's the thing — FODMAP is just an acronym. Six letters, each one a type of carb that can stir up trouble in a sensitive gut. Let's break it down.

F

Fermentable

Stuff that ferments in your gut like a tiny brewery you didn't ask for.

O

Oligosaccharides

Garlic, onion, wheat — the social butterflies that cause chaos. Think of them like a Russian oligarch of your gut: powerful, disruptive, impossible to ignore.

D

Disaccharides

Lactose. Milk's personality.

M

Monosaccharides

Fructose — honey, mango, apple, pear. Too much of it overwhelms your gut.

A

And

Yes, literally just "and." They named a diet after the word "and."

P

Polyols

Stone fruits, cauliflower, mushrooms, sugar-free gum.

Think of it like dating flags — but for your gut.

Here's what's usually fine, what needs a second thought, and what your gut is already writing a strongly-worded letter about.

🟢

Green flag

Go off, your gut is fine

  • plain meat, chicken, fish, eggs
  • rice, oats, quinoa
  • carrots, potatoes, spinach, courgette
  • blueberries, strawberries, firm banana
  • lactose-free milk, almond milk, hard cheese
🟡

Yellow flag

Portion size is everything here

  • sourdough bread (small slice)
  • oats in bigger portions
  • some cheeses like brie or camembert
  • well-rinsed tinned chickpeas, small amounts
  • avocado (⅛ is fine, half is not)
🔴

Red flag

Your gut has filed a restraining order

  • garlic, onion, leek, spring onion
  • wheat, rye, regular pasta
  • milk, soft cheese, yoghurt
  • apple, pear, mango, watermelon, dried fruit
  • honey, beans, most lentils

Portion size changes everything — even a "safe" food can tip over if you eat a lot. This is a plain-language starting point, not a clinical chart. For exact amounts, the Monash FODMAP app is the gold standard, and always check with your doctor or dietitian.
